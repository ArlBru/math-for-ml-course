Before the algorithms: four ideas everything else stands on

Every lesson in this course leans on a handful of statistical ideas so basic they're rarely explained — they're just assumed. Assumed ideas are the most dangerous kind, because nobody ever checks whether you actually understand them. So before Lesson 1, four foundations. Five minutes. Then everything downstream — regression's confidence intervals, K-means' convergence, Bayesian posteriors, even why neural network weight initialisation matters — will have somewhere to stand.

Galton's Quincunx ("Bean Machine"), c. 1894 — the same Galton from Lesson 1
Drop balls through a grid of random left/right pegs — the pile at the bottom is always a bell curve, no matter how the individual balls bounce
Jacob Bernoulli, Ars Conjectandi ("The Art of Conjecturing"), published posthumously, 1713 Jacob Bernoulli spent over 20 years proving something that feels obvious once stated: flip a fair coin a few times and you might get 8 heads in a row — but flip it a million times, and the proportion of heads will creep arbitrarily close to exactly 50%. He called this his "golden theorem." Today it has a less poetic name: the Law of Large Numbers. Individual outcomes are noisy and unpredictable. Averages over many outcomes are not.
As n → ∞,   (1/n)Σxᵢ → E[X]

Why every algorithm in this course needs it: K-means recomputes a centroid as the average of its points — Bernoulli's golden theorem is why that average stabilises into a meaningful centre rather than jittering forever. Gradient descent on mini-batches works because the average gradient over a batch approximates the true gradient over all your data. Bayesian posteriors sharpen as more evidence arrives for exactly this reason. Every time an ML algorithm "learns from data," it is quietly leaning on Bernoulli's 20-year proof that more data converges to truth.

Pierre-Simon Laplace, Théorie Analytique des Probabilités, 1810 — formalising the Central Limit Theorem Look back at the quincunx illustration above. Each ball's path is a sequence of coin-flip-like left/right bounces — pure chance, no two paths alike. And yet the pile at the bottom is, every single time, the same smooth bell curve. This is the Central Limit Theorem: the sum (or average) of many small, independent random effects always tends toward a Normal distribution — regardless of what the individual effects look like. Laplace proved it for sums of any distribution at all, not just coin flips. It is arguably the single most important fact in all of statistics.
X̄ₙ → N(μ, σ²/n)   as n → ∞

Why every algorithm in this course needs it: this is the reason Gauss's bell curve (Lesson 6, PCA) shows up everywhere in nature and data — not because individual measurements are "naturally" bell-shaped, but because they're sums of many small independent errors, and the CLT forces the sum toward a bell shape regardless. It's why linear regression's residuals are assumed Normal, why confidence intervals work at all, and why a neural network's weights, initialised as sums of small random contributions, behave predictably at scale. The quincunx is a toy. The CLT is why the toy's lesson scales to everything.

Florence Nightingale, "Diagram of the Causes of Mortality in the Army in the East", 1858 Florence Nightingale — yes, the nurse — was also a pioneering statistician. Her famous "rose diagram" didn't just show that soldiers were dying in the Crimean War; it showed that far more were dying of preventable disease than of battle wounds, and the visualisation made the spread of causes impossible to ignore or argue away. She understood something crucial: the average death rate told you almost nothing. It was the spread — how causes of death varied — that revealed where action was needed. That spread has a name: variance, and its square root, standard deviation.
σ² = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ − μ)²     σ = √σ²

Why every algorithm in this course needs it: PCA (Lesson 6) literally finds the axis of maximum variance — Nightingale's "spread that matters" generalised to many dimensions. Regularisation (Lesson 8) penalises weights whose variance across features would otherwise let the model overfit to noise. SVM margins (Lesson 7) and Bayesian uncertainty (Lesson 9) are both, underneath, statements about spread — how confident can we be, given how much things vary? Nightingale's insight — averages lie, spread tells the truth — is the quiet engine behind half this course.

Francis Anscombe, "Graphs in Statistical Analysis", The American Statistician, 1973 Statistician Francis Anscombe constructed four datasets — now called Anscombe's Quartet — that have nearly identical summary statistics: same mean, same variance, same correlation coefficient (r ≈ 0.82), even the same regression line. Plot them, and they look nothing alike: one is a clean linear relationship, one is a tight curve, one is linear-with-an-outlier, and one is a vertical line of points plus a single influential outlier. Anscombe's point, made in 1973 and still the first lesson of every good statistics course: a correlation coefficient tells you the strength and direction of a linear relationship — and nothing whatsoever about why it exists, or whether the relationship is even linear at all.
r = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / √(Σ(xᵢ−x̄)²Σ(yᵢ−ȳ)²)

Why every algorithm in this course needs it: Galton's regression line (Lesson 1) is built directly from r — but Anscombe's Quartet is the permanent warning label on that line: a good fit is not the same as a true model, and correlation is not causation. Every "feature importance" score, every PCA loading, every attention weight (Lesson 12) is, at heart, a correlation — a measure of how two things move together, with no built-in claim about why. Keep Anscombe's Quartet in mind every time a model hands you a confident number.

Four ideas, four centuries, one thread: chance is noisy one observation at a time, and astonishingly orderly in aggregate — but "orderly" is not the same as "causal," and "fits well" is not the same as "true." Hold onto those tensions. They resurface in every lesson that follows.

Day 1 · Linear Algebra

Linear Regression

Subject: Francis Galton measured 928 sons. He accidentally invented ML.
After Galton's original scatter diagram, "Regression Towards Mediocrity in Hereditary Stature", 1886
The first regression line ever drawn — Galton called it "mediocrity." We built a field on it.
Francis Galton, Journal of the Anthropological Institute, 1886 In 1886, Victorian polymath Francis Galton plotted the heights of 928 adult sons against their fathers. He noticed something strange: the tallest fathers tended to have shorter sons, and the shortest fathers tended to have taller sons. Everything regressed toward the average — toward mediocrity, as he put it. He called it "regression" as an insult. We kept the word and built an entire field on the line he drew.

Before neural networks. Before transformers. Before anyone used the word "AI" at a dinner party — there was a line through a scatter of Victorian height measurements. And it still works. Embarrassingly well, surprisingly often.

ŷ = mx + b     MSE = (1/n) × Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
The Diagonal of the Unit Square — after the legend of Hippasus, c. 500 BC
The proof that √2 is irrational — said to have cost its discoverer his life at sea
Ian Stewart, 17 Equations That Changed the World, Ch. 1 — Pythagoras' Theorem Stewart opens his book with a 4,000-year-old equation and a death threat. The Pythagoreans believed all numbers were ratios of whole numbers — until one of their own, Hippasus, proved that √2 (the diagonal of a unit square) couldn't be written that way. Legend says his fellow cultists drowned him for it. The lesson buried in that legend is the same one buried in MSE: reality doesn't owe you tidy ratios. Galton's height data wasn't a clean line either — it was a cloud of irrational scatter, and the genius of regression was finding the slope that best survived contact with messy reality, Hippasus-proof.

The painting: Hokusai's The Great Wave off Kanagawa (c. 1831) is built on a hidden golden-ratio spiral — an irrational number organising chaos into composition. That's regression's whole trick: an irrational slope (m = 1.83, not a tidy 2) imposing order on a scatter of points that individually look like nothing but noise. The wave looks chaotic. The spiral underneath it isn't.
Katsushika Hokusai, The Great Wave off Kanagawa, c. 1831 — woodblock print, public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art)

Every data point is a stubborn Victorian gentleman standing in a field. Your line is a rope. Each man holds a vertical elastic band connecting him to the rope. MSE is the total tension. Galton's insight was finding the rope position that minimises that tension — and the red lines in the visual above are those elastic bands.

Code snippet
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
import numpy as np

X = np.array([1,2,3,4,5]).reshape(-1,1)
y = np.array([2.1,3.9,6.2,7.8,10.1])

Day 2 · Optimisation

Gradient Descent

Subject: A blind man on a mountain. That's your model learning.
The Blind Man Descending the Mountain — after the Zen kōan tradition, Rinzai school, 12th century
"Not knowing is most intimate." Feel the slope. Take one step downhill. That is the entire algorithm.
Zen kōan, attributed to the Rinzai tradition, 12th century There is a Zen kōan: a student asks the master how to find the path. The master says: feel the ground beneath your foot. Take one step in the direction it slopes downward. Then feel again. The student asks: but what if I never see the whole mountain? The master says: you don't need to see the mountain. You need to feel the next step. Your model is that student.

Gradient descent powers virtually every ML model you'll ever train. The update rule — feel the slope, take one step:

θ = θ − α × ∇L(θ)
The Calculus Priority Dispute — Newton vs. Leibniz, Royal Society, 1712
Two notations for the same derivative — and decades of bitterness over who got there first
Ian Stewart, 17 Equations That Changed the World, Ch. 2 — Calculus In the 1670s, Newton and Leibniz independently invented the derivative — the mathematics of "the slope right here, right now" — and then spent the rest of their lives accusing each other of plagiarism. The Royal Society (presided over by Newton, conveniently) ran an "impartial" inquiry and ruled in Newton's favour. Three centuries of bitterness, over a tool whose entire purpose is captured by the Zen master's instruction: feel the slope, take one step. Every gradient descent step you run is, quite literally, the Newton–Leibniz feud settled a billion times a second on a GPU.

The painting: William Blake's Newton (1795) shows the man crouched on a rock at the bottom of the sea, bent entirely over a single diagram, compass in hand, measuring one curve while ignoring the vast unknown darkness around him. Blake meant it as a criticism — too much focus on one local calculation, too little awareness of the whole. It's an uncannily exact picture of gradient descent: a single point on the loss surface, compass out, measuring only the slope right under its feet, blind to the rest of the landscape by design.
William Blake, Newton, 1795 — colour print, Tate Gallery, public domain

Where α is the learning rate (how big a step the blind man takes). Too large: he overshoots the valley. Too small: he's still on the mountain when you need results. The illustration above is that mountain at night — the purple curve is the loss landscape, the dashed amber trail is the path already walked, and the coral dot is the blind man's foot, feeling only the ground directly beneath it. The interactive visual below is the same scene, just zoomed in and put under your control: hit Step and watch the dot descend exactly as the kōan describes — one felt step at a time, never seeing the whole mountain.

Draw a wavy curve — your loss landscape. Mark a starting point on a slope. Draw three paths: huge steps (bouncing wildly), tiny steps (glacial), just right (lands in the valley). Label them. You've drawn every bad training run you've ever had — and the Zen master would approve of only one.
Code snippet
def gradient_descent(x, y, lr=0.01, epochs=100):
    m, b = 0, 0
    n = len(x)
    for _ in range(epochs):
        y_pred = m * x + b
        dm = (-2/n) * sum(x * (y - y_pred))  # feel the slope
        db = (-2/n) * sum(y - y_pred)
        m -= lr * dm  # take one step
        b -= lr * db
    return m, b

import numpy as np
x = np.array([1,2,3,4,5], dtype=float)
y = np.array([2,4,5,4,5], dtype=float)
print(gradient_descent(x, y))
# Try lr=0.5 — watch the blind man fall off the mountain

Day 3 · Classification

Logistic Regression

Subject: Odysseus stood at the threshold. Your model does it a million times a day.
Odysseus at the Gates of Hades — after Homer's Odyssey, Book XI, c. 8th century BC
Maximum uncertainty at the threshold. One step either way collapses the probability to 0 or 1.
Homer, The Odyssey, Book XI — Nekuia (The Book of the Dead) In Book XI of The Odyssey, Odysseus sails to the edge of the known world — the boundary between the living and the dead. He stands at the threshold: one step forward is the underworld, one step back is life. He is precisely at 0.5 on the sigmoid — maximum uncertainty, frozen at the decision boundary. The gods had already set the threshold. Logistic regression just learned it from data.

Linear regression predicts a number. But sometimes you need a decision — spam or not spam, fraud or legitimate, living world or underworld. The sigmoid function squashes any raw score into a probability between 0 and 1:

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ᶻ)
The Logistic Map's Bifurcation Diagram — Robert May, Nature, 1976
One equation, one dial (k) — turn it past 3.0 and order dissolves into chaos
Ian Stewart, Weirder Maths / The Mathematics of Chaos — the Logistic Map The sigmoid has a wilder cousin: the logistic map, xₙ₊₁ = kxₙ(1−xₙ), first written down by Pierre Verhulst in 1838 to model population growth with a limit. In the 1970s, biologist Robert May discovered that turning the dial k just slightly past a certain point sends this gentle S-shaped equation into total chaos — population numbers that never repeat, never settle, and depend so sensitively on the starting value that two populations differing by a billionth of a person diverge completely within 50 generations. Same family, same equation lineage as your sigmoid — except logistic regression lives in the calm, well-behaved region of the dial, while its chaotic cousin lives one step away, at the edge of the gate.

The painting: Jackson Pollock's drip paintings (e.g. No. 5, 1948) look random, but fractal analysis has shown they have measurable structure — Pollock's arm motion was a deterministic process producing what looks like noise. That's the logistic map past k = 3.57: a completely deterministic equation, no randomness anywhere in the formula, producing trajectories that look exactly like a Pollock canvas. Logistic regression's sigmoid is Pollock before he picked up the brush — same hand, same equation, calm region of the dial.
🎨 Jackson Pollock, No. 5, 1948 — still under copyright, so not reproduced here. View it via the Museum of Modern Art collection or search "Pollock No. 5 1948" — look for the dense, deterministic-looking tangle of drips, then picture the logistic map's bifurcation diagram from a few lines above. Same texture, same underlying idea: simple rules, repeated, producing apparent chaos.

Day 4 · Ensemble methods

Decision Trees & Random Forests

Subject: Borges wrote the decision tree in 1941. We just didn't know it.
After Borges' Garden of Forking Paths — Ficciones, 1941
Every node is a fork. Every leaf is a world. At depth 5 the labyrinth becomes impenetrable.
Jorge Luis Borges, "El jardín de senderos que se bifurcan", Ficciones, 1941 In Borges' story, a Chinese spy discovers his ancestor had written a labyrinthine novel in which every possible outcome of every decision occurs simultaneously — branching endlessly, every fork spawning new worlds. Borges called it "an infinite series of times, in a dizzying net of divergent, convergent and parallel times." He was describing, in 1941, precisely how a decision tree works. At each node, the data forks. Each branch is a different world. You keep splitting until the worlds are pure.

Decision trees are the most human algorithm in ML — a flowchart with a maths degree. The magic is in which questions to ask, measured by entropy — the impurity of each node:

H = −Σ pᵢ log₂(pᵢ)
Claude Shannon's Bit, Bell Labs, 1948
The unicycling engineer who quantified uncertainty — and gave decision trees their splitting rule
Claude Shannon, "A Mathematical Theory of Communication", Bell System Technical Journal, 1948 In 1948, an engineer at Bell Labs who liked to ride a unicycle through the halls while juggling published a paper that invented the "bit" and, with it, the equation H = −Σpᵢlog₂pᵢ — a measure of how much uncertainty (or "surprise") is packed into a message. Shannon was solving a telephone-line problem. Eight decades later, that exact formula is what a decision tree computes at every single split: how much surprise is left in this group of data, and which question removes the most of it? Borges' forking garden and Shannon's noisy telephone wire turn out to be the same equation, measuring the same thing — uncertainty, quantified.

The painting: Piet Mondrian's Composition with Red, Blue and Yellow (1930) is the result of repeatedly splitting a canvas with horizontal and vertical lines until each region holds one flat, pure colour — no gradients, no ambiguity, maximum visual "purity" per cell. That is, almost cell-for-cell, what a decision tree does to a feature space: split, split again, until each region is as close to one class — one colour — as possible. Mondrian was minimising entropy with a ruler decades before Shannon gave the impurity a name.
🎨 Piet Mondrian, Composition with Red, Blue and Yellow, 1930 — still under copyright in some jurisdictions, so not reproduced here. Search "Mondrian Composition 1930" — note the canvas divided into rectangles by horizontal/vertical lines, each rectangle one flat colour. That grid of pure, separated regions is visually identical to a decision tree's partition of feature space, drawn 11 years before Shannon and decades before "decision tree" was a term.

Day 5 · Unsupervised learning

K-Means Clustering

Subject: Plato's cave, centroids, and why your customers fall into three types
Plato's Allegory of the Cave — The Republic, c. 375 BC, Book VII
Prisoners see only shadows. The centroid is the ideal Form casting the shadow. K-means finds it.
Plato, The Republic, c. 375 BC — Allegory of the Cave, Book VII Prisoners chained in a cave see only shadows on the wall — projections of real objects they can never directly observe. The shadows are the data. The real objects casting them are the underlying clusters — the ideal Forms. The philosopher who escapes and sees the real objects? That's K-means. It finds the centroid — the ideal Form — that each shadow is an imperfect copy of.

K-means is unsupervised learning: no labels, no shadows to point at. Just data and the question: are there natural groups hiding here, and what are their ideal Forms?

d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
After the Manga Guide to Linear Algebra — distance in n dimensions
Same Pythagorean formula your centroid-distance calculation uses, just with more dimensions under the root
Manga Guide to Linear Algebra — on Pythagoras' Theorem in n dimensions The Manga Guide to Linear Algebra opens its distance chapter with a student baffled that the same little right-triangle formula from school somehow still works in 10, 100, or 10,000 dimensions — you just keep adding squared differences under the root. Every time K-means measures "how far is this customer from that centroid," it's running Pythagoras' 2,500-year-old theorem, just with more terms under the square root. Plato's prisoners measuring distance to the fire and a recommendation engine measuring distance to a cluster centroid are doing exactly the same arithmetic — only the number of dimensions changed.

The painting: Picasso's Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (1907) shows faces from multiple viewpoints simultaneously — front and profile fused into one figure, as if the canvas were a flattened projection of a higher-dimensional object. That's precisely the conceptual leap K-means makes routinely: a "customer" might be a point in 40-dimensional space (age, spend, clicks, location...), and the centroid distance between two customers is a single number summarising all 40 viewpoints at once. Picasso fractured one face into many angles; K-means collapses many angles into one distance.
🎨 Pablo Picasso, Les Demoiselles d'Avignon, 1907 — still under copyright, so not reproduced here. View it via the MoMA collection or search the title — note how each face shows front and side simultaneously, multiple "views" compressed into one. That compression — many perspectives, one representation — is exactly what a centroid does to a 40-dimensional customer profile.

Day 6 · Dimensionality reduction

PCA — Principal Component Analysis

Subject: A 2D creature discovers the third dimension. That's PCA.
After Edwin Abbott's Flatland — A Romance of Many Dimensions, 1884
A. Square lives in 2D and cannot perceive height. The Sphere shows him the shape of his world from above. That is PCA.
Edwin Abbott Abbott, Flatland: A Romance of Many Dimensions, 1884 A. Square is a two-dimensional being who cannot perceive height. When a three-dimensional Sphere passes through Flatland, A. Square sees only a circle that appears and disappears mysteriously. Then the Sphere lifts him out of the plane — and for the first time he sees the true shape of his world from above. PCA is that vertical lift. It picks you up out of the plane you've been trapped in and shows you which directions your data actually varies along.

Your dataset has 100 features. You can't visualise them. PCA finds the directions of maximum variance and projects everything onto those directions. Eigenvectors and eigenvalues of the covariance matrix do the work:

PC1 = argmax ‖Xw‖²   subject to ‖w‖ = 1
Gauss and the Bell Curve — the old 10-Deutschmark note, in circulation 1991–2001
Derived while hunting a lost asteroid; later became the only equation ever to appear on currency
Ian Stewart, 17 Equations That Changed the World, Ch. 8 — The Normal Distribution Carl Friedrich Gauss derived the bell curve while trying to predict where a lost asteroid, Ceres, would reappear from behind the sun — and Germany later put his face and his bell curve on the 10-Deutschmark note, the only equation ever to become literal currency. The bell curve describes how errors scatter around a true value in every direction. PCA's whole job is to take that scattered cloud — Gauss's bell curve, but stretched into an ellipse — and find the axis along which it's most stretched. A. Square's Sphere and Gauss's asteroid are doing the same trick: finding the one direction where the "error cloud" reveals the most about the true shape underneath.

The painting: Georges Seurat's A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte (1884–86) is built from thousands of individual dots of pure colour — up close, pure noise; from a distance, a coherent scene snaps into focus along certain viewing directions more than others. PCA does the same thing to a data cloud: thousands of individual points, each meaningless alone, but step back along the right axis (PC1) and the "scene" — the dominant pattern — resolves. Seurat chose his viewing distance by eye. PCA chooses its axis by eigenvalue.
Georges Seurat, A Sunday on La Grande Jatte, 1884–86 — oil on canvas, Art Institute of Chicago, public domain

Code snippet
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

After Miyamoto Musashi's Go Rin No Sho (The Book of Five Rings), 1645 — the concept of ma
The secret of combat is the interval — the gap between opponents. Musashi called it ma. SVMs maximise it.
Miyamoto Musashi, The Book of Five Rings (Go Rin No Sho), 1645 Miyamoto Musashi, the greatest swordsman in Japanese history, wrote that the secret of combat was not to strike at the opponent directly but to find the ma — the space between, the interval, the gap. Maximise: 2/‖w‖    subject to: yᵢ(w·xᵢ + b) ≥ 1

Subject: A medieval friar invented the best ML technique in 1323.
After the Summa Logicae, William of Ockham, c. 1323 — Ockham's Razor
The simplest model that fits the data is the best model. L1 and L2 are this principle in calculus.
William of Ockham, Franciscan friar and philosopher, c. 1287–1347 William of Ockham was a 14th century Franciscan friar who spent most of his life in trouble with the Pope. In between escaping papal authority, he articulated what became the most important principle in all of science: "Entities must not be multiplied beyond necessity." The simplest model that explains the data is the best model. He was describing regularisation in 1323, seven centuries before your model started memorising the training set.

Your model hits 99% on training data. Then 61% on test. Ockham would have seen it coming. Regularisation adds a penalty that punishes complexity — L2 shrinks all weights, L1 slices some to exactly zero:

L₂: Loss + λΣwᵢ²    |    L₁: Loss + λΣ|wᵢ|

Drag lambda. Watch L1 (coral) drive weights to exactly zero — Ockham's razor cutting away unnecessary entities. L2 (purple) just trims, never quite cutting all the way through.

Neural Networks & Backpropagation

Subject: Ada Lovelace said machines could only do what we tell them. She was almost right.
Ada Lovelace, Notes on the Analytical Engine, Note A, 1843
"The Analytical Engine has no power of originating anything." Neural networks are the 180-year rebuttal.
Ada Lovelace, translator's Notes on Menabrea's "Sketch of the Analytical Engine", Note A, 1843 In 1843, Ada Lovelace translated an Italian article about Babbage's Analytical Engine and added her own notes — longer and more insightful than the original. In Note A, she wrote: "The Analytical Engine has no power of originating anything. It can only do what we know how to order it to perform." She was right about Babbage's machine. Neural networks are the 180-year rebuttal — a machine that learns, from data, patterns nobody explicitly wrote down.

A neural network is linear regression + gradient descent + logistic regression + regularisation, stacked and composed. Each neuron computes a weighted sum of its inputs, then passes through a non-linear activation:

z = Wx + b  →  a = ReLU(z) = max(0,z)

Training uses backpropagation — the chain rule applied backwards through every layer, computing each weight's contribution to the loss. Lovelace's machine, finally originating:

∂L/∂w = ∂L/∂a × ∂a/∂z × ∂z/∂w

Look back at the illustration above: on the left, Babbage's Analytical Engine — Lovelace's actual subject in 1843, all fixed gears and pre-determined columns, exactly as rigid as her quote describes. On the right, the small purple-green-coral network — the structure her words couldn't conceive of, because nothing about it is "ordered to perform" in the Engine's sense; the connections themselves are learned. The interactive visual below lets you grow that right-hand network — add hidden layers, add neurons per layer — and watch the web of connections multiply. Every one of those faint grey lines is a weight that backpropagation will adjust on its own, Lovelace's "originating" made literal.

Day 11 · Computer vision

CNNs — Convolutional Neural Networks

Subject: How Monet painted and how your image classifier sees — same mechanism
After Claude Monet, Water Lilies (Nymphéas), Musée de l'Orangerie, Paris, 1896–1926
Monet didn't paint the pond. He painted local patches of it — 250 times. CNNs do the same thing, faster.
Claude Monet, Water Lilies series (Nymphéas), Giverny, 1896–1926 — Musée de l'Orangerie, Paris Monet didn't paint the Giverny pond. He painted the light falling on patches of it — each canvas a small window of attention sliding across the surface, capturing local texture, colour and movement rather than the scene as a whole. By the end of his life he had painted 250 variations of the same local patches. A CNN does precisely this: a small filter slides across the image patch by patch, extracting local features at each position. Monet was running convolutions. He just called it painting.

If you feed a raw image into a standard neural network, you flatten it into a vector — a 256×256 image becomes 65,536 numbers. This destroys all spatial structure. CNNs fix this by sliding a filter patch by patch, like Monet at his easel:

Feature map[i,j] = Σ Σ Filter[m,n] × Image[i+m, j+n]
Fourier's Heat Obsession and the Convolution Theorem, 1822
"Théorie analytique de la chaleur" — the wave decomposition that secretly powers every CNN filter
Ian Stewart, 17 Equations That Changed the World, Ch. 7 — The Wave Equation / Fourier Transform Joseph Fourier was so obsessed with heat — convinced it was the secret to health — that he wrapped himself in blankets indoors even in summer, and some historians blame this habit for the fall that killed him in 1830. His obsession produced the Fourier transform: any signal can be broken into a sum of simple waves. The convolution theorem — Fourier's quiet legacy — proves that sliding a filter across an image (what a CNN does) is mathematically the same operation as multiplying frequencies together in Fourier space. Monet's sliding canvas and Fourier's wrapped-up obsession with heat waves are, underneath the notation, the exact same equation wearing different coats.

The painting: Wassily Kandinsky's Composition VIII (1923) decomposes a scene into circles, lines, and triangles repeated at different scales and orientations — a visual vocabulary of basic shapes that combine to build complexity. That's a CNN's filter bank made literal: early layers learn small repeated motifs (edges, curves, corners — Kandinsky's circles and lines), and deeper layers combine those motifs into faces, objects, scenes. Kandinsky believed every complex image could be built from a small alphabet of basic forms. He was, accidentally, describing the layer-by-layer logic of convolutional feature hierarchies a century early.
Wassily Kandinsky, Composition VIII, 1923 — oil on canvas, Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum, public domain (pre-1928)

class MonetCNN(nn.Module):

Attention & Transformers

Subject: Proust spent 3,000 pages doing what attention does in one matrix multiplication
After Marcel Proust's madeleine passage — À la recherche du temps perdu, Vol. I, 1913
One taste summons a whole world. One query token attends to all keys with learned weights. Same mechanism.
Marcel Proust, In Search of Lost Time (À la recherche du temps perdu), Vol. I: Swann's Way, 1913 In the most famous passage of Proust's seven-volume novel, the narrator dips a madeleine biscuit into tea and is suddenly, involuntarily transported back to his childhood in Combray — not because he tried to remember it, but because that taste weighted every other memory in his mind and the most relevant one surfaced at full intensity. Proust called it involuntary memory. We call it the attention mechanism. He needed 3,000 pages. The transformer does it in one matrix multiplication.

The transformer — behind GPT, BERT, DALL-E, Whisper, AlphaFold — is built on one central idea: every token attends to every other token simultaneously, weighted by relevance. Proust's madeleine, generalised:

Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) × V

Q (Query) = the madeleine. K (Key) = every memory in the narrator's mind. V (Value) = Combray, surfacing at full weight. Select a query token below — watch Proust's involuntary weights fan out.

Code snippet
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

def attention(Q, K, V):
    d_k = Q.shape[-1]
    scores  = Q @ K.transpose(-2,-1) / d_k**0.5  # the taste of the madeleine
    weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)              # involuntary weights
    return weights @ V, weights                        # Combray surfaces

seq_len, d_model = 6, 8  # "The cat sat on the mat"
Q = torch.randn(1, seq_len, d_model)
K = torch.randn(1, seq_len, d_model)
V = torch.randn(1, seq_len, d_model)

output, w = attention(Q, K, V)
print("Output:", output.shape)
print("Weights:\n", w[0].detach().round(decimals=2))
# Each row sums to 1 — Proust's memories, normalised
